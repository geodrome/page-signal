Melting polar cap No. 1 weather story of 2007

CanWest News Service

OTTAWA -- The disappearance of Arctic sea ice-- reported in September-- was Environment Canadaâ??s No. 1 weather story of 2007.

In fact, the United Nations declared the record loss of ice as one of the worldâ??s biggest events. The thinning and shrinking of the ice, largely a result of a series of consecutive warm years, has had a profound impact on the North-- people, plants and wildlife alike.

Also dominating this yearâ??s top weather stories were menacing floods in British Columbia, No. 2 on the list. The threat of flooding hung over thousands of residents for months. But while devastating floods occurred in the central interior and north coast, people along the Fraser River were spared when a major storm changed directions at the last moment.

Luck was also a factor in Elie, Man., when Canadaâ??s first documented F-5 intensity tornado, with winds above 420 kilometres per hour, touched down on June 22, making No. 10 on the list.

Environment Canada ranks its top weather events from one to 10 based on factors that include the impact they had on Canada and Canadians, the extent of the area affected, economic effects and longevity as a top news story.

At times in 2007, the West had too much weather. Residents on the Prairies witnessed a record number of severe summer weather warnings, with tornadoes, intense rainfalls, wind storms and hail storms.

There was also the shocker of a green Christmas Day in 2006 in Quebec City, Timmins and Thunder Bay, Ont.-- where a white Christmas is all but guaranteed.

In Atlantic Canada, one of the big stories was the passage of Hurricane Noel in November. While no Juan, Noel destroyed several beaches, wharves and docks.

It was another warm year for Canada, the 11th year in a row. The year tied for the second warmest winter on record, some three degrees warmer than normal. Summer was the seventh warmest, at about one degree warmer than usual, and from January to November the national average temperature was around one degree above normal.
