Phishers on the hunt for ?mules?
Mahalakshmi Hariharan
Saturday, December 29, 2007  03:05 IST

People are hired as money transfer agents and made to forward stolen funds

MUMBAI: Another online banking scam has arrived in India, where unsuspecting people are made to transfer stolen money for fraudsters, in what is being called a money mule operation. Banks have gotten cautious, after such an incident was noticed at an Indian bank recently.

The scam is closely related to ?phishing?, where fraudsters use a fake email and look-alike website to get customers to ?sign in? to their bank accounts, and record the information entered.

Here?s how a money mule operation works: The fraudster first steals bank account details of unwary customers through a ?phishing? scam. He then has the money transferred to his account by a third person, or the ?mule?, who is made to access the hacked bank account.

The fraudsters recruit ?money mules? through spam emails, newsletters, advertisements in newspapers, online job websites and chat rooms. They pose as ?financial managers? or other top level functionaries for money transfer companies and make it very clear that no special knowledge or skill sets are required to do the job - all one has to do is ?remit? funds as and when the orders come.

Once a mule transfers the money into the fraudsters account - usually in a different country - the fraudster withdraws the money, closes the account and flees.

?Money mule is the other side of a phishing operation that most people never see or hear about. Without a mule, fraudsters really can?t do anything with stolen credit card credentials,? said Amuleek Bijral country manager - India & SAARC with RSA, the Security Division of EMC. ?Money mule scams are very common in the US and the UK.?
