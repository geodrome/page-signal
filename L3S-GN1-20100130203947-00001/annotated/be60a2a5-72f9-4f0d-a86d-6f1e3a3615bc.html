Concern over destroyed embryos by IVF clinics



By Laura Clout

Last Updated: 12:44am GMT  31/12/2007


More than one million "leftover" embryos created for fertility treatment have been destroyed over the past 14 years, official statistics show.

Data from the Human Fertilisation and Embryology Authority show that of 2,137,924 embryos created in British clinics between 1991 and 2005, about 1.2 million were never used. Just four per cent resulted in live births.

Most of these spare embryos, created to maximise the chances of a viable pregnancy, are destroyed within days.

Some will be frozen for future use and a small number are donated for research. An even tinier number are donated to other couples.

Lord Alton, the independent peer who obtained the figures in answer to a parliamentary question, said the wastage rate was "phenomenal".

He referred to an influential 1984 report that effectively gave approval for embryo research.

"We were told by Lady Warnock [who chaired the committee that produced the report] that human embryos would be given respect and special status.

"I would go further to claim that the sanctity of human life attaches to human embryos. But when you are flushing embryos down the drain it doesn't demonstrate much respect," Lord Alton said.

"It shows how quickly we move from the positions that we take to the point where we say it doesn't matter whether we destroy millions of human embryos for no gain."

About 30,000 women undergo IVF treatment every year, although the live birth rate is, at best, only 28 per cent for women under 35.

The figures reveal that the embryos created between 1991 and 2005 resulted in only 85,800 live births during the period. However, many embryos will have been stored for use in future IVF cycles.

Politicians and campaign groups say that fertility patients should be encouraged to donate spare embryos to childless couples for "adoption" - a process that is legal, but very rare.

Josephine Quintvalle, the co-founder of Comment on Reproductive Ethics, said: "It is already possible for couples to donate embryos [for adoption] but perhaps they should be made more aware of the possibility of doing so."

Bob Spink, the Conservative MP and a member of the science and technology committee, said: "First of all we've got to understand the anguish that is caused to families who cannot have children and do all we can to help them.

"But at the same time we've got to have respect for human life. At the moment there are too many embryos that are created and then destroyed.

"I think that the industry, the medical profession, have to think very carefully about their creation."

Earlier this year, a US biotechnology firm sparked an ethics row by encouraging couples to use leftover embryos as a personalised family "repair kit".

Leading scientists accused StemLifeLine of exploitation after it offered IVF patients the chance to extract stem cells from the embryos in the hope that they could be used to treat life-threatening conditions such as heart disease.
