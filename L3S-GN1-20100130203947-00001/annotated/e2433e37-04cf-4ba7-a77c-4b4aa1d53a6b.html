Struggle to contain WA bushfire

By Liza Kappelle and Nicolas Perpitch

A bushfire that killed three people after a truck convoy was caught in the inferno has continued to rage out of control in the West Australian Goldfields.

The bodies of two adults and a child were found in two burnt-out trucks on the Great Eastern Highway in a section of Boorabbin National Park between Southern Cross and Coolgardie about midnight (WDT).

Police spokesman Ian Hasleby said it was unclear how the nine-truck convoy became trapped.

But he said seven prime movers escaped after drivers dumped their trailers 75km east of Southern Cross and headed back to Coolgardie.

"They dumped their trailers off because they couldn't turn around," Mr Hasleby said.

Police have been unable to examine the three bodies beyond a cursory glance because of the dangerous conditions.

"We still don't know who they are," Mr Haselby said.

"The fire is still burning out that way, they have to be very careful, it is a very dangerous situation."

The highway was closed most of on Sunday, with traffic only allowed through in convoys under police or Department of Environment and Conservation (DEC) escort until the road was reopened about 6pm.

A DEC spokeswoman said the road was again closed on Monday, with firefighters struggling to contain the wildfire which has destroyed more than 10,000 hectares since Friday.

"We have got aerial surveillance underway to determine the extend of the fire and the terrain that it has gone into," she said.

"We are working on the western boundary north and south of Great Eastern Highway and we have heavy machinery and trucks going in to help."

Lee Lewis, manager of the Caltex Southern Cross roadhouse, said the mood of the town was very sombre.

"Most people coming in are subdued. At the moment, the town's just waking up and finding out about it," Mr Lewis said.

The winds on Sunday night were very strong, he said.

"The wind was crazy yesterday and last night, it was very gusty, blowing a gale. It can quickly change direction on people."

Trucks heading interstate were banking up in Southern Cross or turning back to Perth, he said.

Education Minister Mark McGowan, speaking on behalf of the state government, offered condolences to the families of those who died.

"It's obviously very distressing and very disturbing and very sad," Mr McGowan said.

"It's a tragedy and as I understand it there's going to be a full coronial inquiry, as there should be, into it.

"All we can say is that for those people who have lost family members we're very sorry and we hope they are coping as well as they can."
