Georgian opposition threatens mass demo over disputed vote

TBILISI (Thomson Financial) - The opposition in Georgia threatened today to flood Tbilisi with demonstrators if the authorities do not overturn the apparent re-election of Mikheil Saakashvili by Sunday.

'On the 13th of January, if we don't get the second round by then, the whole of Georgia will be out on the streets,' senior opposition leader Salome Zurabishvili told a press conference.

The ex-Soviet republic's opposition claims that Saturday's presidential election was rigged to ensure Saakashvili won in a first round so as to avoid a tense run-off against a united opposition candidate.

The CEC had yet to release final results of the vote by Tuesday evening, three days after polls closed.

With about 90 pct of ballots counted, Saakashvili had 52.1 pct -- clearing the 50 pct barrier for winning in a single round. Main opposition leader Levan Gachechiladze, his nearest competitor, had 24.98 pct.

CEC Chairman Levan Tarkhnishvili declared late Sunday that an unofficial final count showed victory was certain for Saakashvili.

The CEC has until Sunday evening -- eight days after the polls closed -- to finish the vote count, officials said.

Zurabishvili, a former foreign minister, said that at least 100,000 opposition votes had been stolen. She condemned declarations by international monitors that the vote was largely fair.

'We deplore that these elections have been qualified by certain countries and certain organisations as democratic elections. This is an insult to the Georgian people,' she said.

Earlier, Gachechiladze dramatically confronted Tarkhnishvili and demanded his resignation.

'Resign! Otherwise we will protest every day,' Gachechiladze yelled at Tarkhnishvili in an incident shown on national television.

'You are telling lies to the whole of Georgia. We demand the resignation of the liar Tarkhnishvili,' shouted Gachechiladze, jabbing his finger at the official.

Tarkhnishvili responded by telling him to take his complaints to official channels or the courts.

'This is pressure and threats,' he complained.

The opposition has 'all possible tools to act within the framework of law,' he said.

Later, at a protest against alleged media bias outside the offices of Georgian Public Television, Gachechiladze addressed a message to Saakashvili and interim president Nino Burdjanadze.

'I will not stop until you kill me,' he said, his face filled with rage.

Justice Minister Ekaterine Tkeshelashvili said the opposition 'must prevent illegal actions, such as attempts to threaten the work of the Central Election Commission.''I call upon the opposition to respect the Georgian people and its choice,' she said in televised comments.

Opposition leaders say they have documentary evidence of fraud and have vowed to challenge the results in court.

One opposition leader, David Usupashvili said Tuesday that it was 'more than unusual and highly suspicious' that complete results have not yet been announced.

Saakashvili -- whose main goal is to wrest Georgia from Russia's influence and win NATO and EU membership -- says that if his renewed five-year mandate is confirmed he will pursue free-market and democratic reforms.

The opposition supports the country's pro-Western course, but accuses Saakashvili of becoming increasingly authoritarian and ignoring the poor in pushing free-market reforms.

Despite opposition allegations of fraud, the conduct of the election got a vote of confidence from the foreign observers of the Organisation for Security and Cooperation in Europe (OSCE).

Saakashvili called the election a year early to try to defuse the crisis that erupted in November when police brutally beat anti-government protestors and he declared a nine-day state of emergency.
