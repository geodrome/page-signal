Schreiber says Mulroney needed money, and assisted his company

Juliet O'Neill and Jack Aubry ,Â  CanWest News Service

Published:Â Thursday, November 29, 2007

OTTAWA - Karlheinz Schreiber told MPs on Thursday the $300,000 he paid Brian Mulroney in the early 1990s came after Fred Doucet told him the then prime minister was in "desperate shape and needed the money."

He told MPs he paid Mulroney in cash because "the cash was available," and he said the money had nothing to do with any pasta business, as Mulroney's spokesmen have said in the past. The purpose of the cash was to pay Mulroney "for future services" and were related to his attempt to get an armoured vehicle plant established for Thyssen Industries, a German arms manufacturer, in Bear Head, N.S.

He also told the ethics committee that he had never met or spoken directly to Prime Minister Stephen Harper, who has ordered a public inquiry into his relationship with Mulroney.

Schreiber said Mulroney promised to bring Harper a letter asking for help with some of Schreiber's legal problems that needed to be addressed such as his citizenship and staying in Canada.

He said Harper and Mulroney did meet to discuss his situation, and he was told the meeting went well.

"The outcome was that the message was very well received," said Schreiber. He said he was told there would be no guarantees.

This contradicts statements by Harper, who said Mulroney never raised Schreiber's affairs with him.

"It was a shock for me when Prime Minister Harper said publicly on television Mr Mulroney never spoke with him about me or my letter."

And he said when he thinks back to how angry he was when he found out Mulroney's government was not prepared to invest in the Bear Head, project "I have a problem not to bring up in front of you."

Schreiber said the amount he discussed with Mulroney was $500,000, but he paid him only $300,000 and was fighting in court to get the money back now from Mulroney because "he didn't perform."

MPs began teasing some answers from Schreiber after he opened an appearance at the House of Commons ethics committee with a statement that he did not want to testify until he is free from jail, secures a delay of extradition and consults his personal documents.

But after rejecting a Conservative motion to delay the hearing until the evening, MPs began asking questions Schreiber about his relationship with Mulroney. Schreiber initially would only respond "defer," indicating he needed to refer to his documents. Eventually he warmed up and answered some lines of questions.

Schreiber provoked laughter when Bloc Quebecois MP Carole Lavallee asked if he would pay her $300,000 if she said she was hard up.

"Under the current circumstances, yes," Schreiber said.

Schreiber said he was told by Doucet, a Mulroney aide, that Mulroney was in "desperate shape and needed money"_and that he should help him. Schreiber also said Mulroney was helpful with one of his companies, Bear Head Industries Ltd., one of Schreiber's four companies based in Port Hawkesbury, N.S.

He formed it in the mid-1980s in connection with Thyssen Industries. Schreiber then lobbied the government for a contract to build armoured vehicles in Bear Head, N.S. The Bear Head project does not appear to have survived past 1993. The company last filed a corporate annual report with Industry Canada in 1995 and has since been dissolved.

Schreiber said Mulroney was also helpful with the reunification of Germany and for those two reasons he met with Mulroney at the prime minister's resident at Harrington Lake, where they concluded an agreement.

At the opening of a hearing on the Hill, Schreiber said he was upset that he was brought to Ottawa from Toronto in leg irons, handcuffs and an orange jumpsuit. He also wanted to return to Toronto for a court hearing Friday dealing with his latest request for a delay in extradition.

"I was not given an opportunity to go to my home, I was not given any opportunity to retrieve any necessary documents and I have not been given any opportunity to review ... in order to properly prepare myself to testify," he said.

"I wish to speak but not in circumstances that are calculated to be degrading and humiliating," he said, adding that he was acting on the advice of lawyer Ed Greenspan.

Committee chairman Paul Szabo proposed that MPs proceed with their questions and Schreiber could answer them immediately or at a hearing next Tuesday after he has had time to prepare.

"We understand you are at a disadvantage right now," Szabo, a Liberal MP, said.

His proposal set off debate among MPs.

Conservative MP David Tilson, vice-chair of the committee, objected to Szabo's "willy nilly" decision and Conservative MP Russ Hiebert proposed to reconvene after the court hearing Friday.

Conservative MP Dean Del Mastro accused Schreiber of indulging in "nothing but an extradition delay tactic."

Bloc Quebecois MP Carole Lavallee objected to Hiebert's "disguised adjournment motion," as did Liberal MP Sukh Dhaliwal.

NDP MP Pat Martin reminded the witness that "he does not have the right to remain silent."

In listing his conditions before he would testify, Schreiber said:

. he wants to have his case before the Ontario Court of Appeal (Friday) to proceed.

. he wants to have the paperwork underway for the Supreme Court appeal, including the period during which the federal government has to respond. This would take things until mid-January.

. he wants time to fully understand what the scope of the committee is.

Szabo said he has outlined all this in a letter to him and to Greenspan, but agreed that can have as much time as he needs to understand all this issues and to speak to them.

Schreiber, who was sworn in, which is considered uncommon, was dressed casually but confined by handcuffs when police escorted him through the freight entrance of the Centre Block on Parliament Hill to appear at the House of Commons ethics committee to testify about his relationship with former Progressive Conservative prime minister Brian Mulroney.

Schreiber's lawyers had been expected to request a postponement of his testimony on grounds he had spent the night incarcerated at the Ottawa-Carleton Detention Centre and was ill-prepared to begin filling in the details of $300,000 in cash payments and years of other dealings with Mulroney that have prompted a separate government-order public inquiry next year.

Schreiber is to return to the committee on Tuesday. Members have recommended that he be placed under house arrest until then, a move that would allow him to reside in his Ottawa home, rather than in a detention centre. The decision rests with the Speaker of the House of Commons.
