Covic turns up heat on Schwarzer

January 18, 2008

A spectacular save can only have helped this keeper's national ambitions, writes Michael Cockerill.

ANTE COVIC was effectively an observer at the last World Cup but he plans to be a player at the next, and mind games by incumbent Mark Schwarzer won't deter him.

Fresh from the save of the A-League season last weekend, the Newcastle Jets custodian is confident of his Socceroos ambitions. Quietly put is his view that Schwarzer's time might be up. We've heard that before, of course. But since the premature retirement of Mark Bosnich in 1999 opened the door, Schwarzer has beaten off all challengers and, at 35, makes no secret of his desire to do it again.

Yet things have changed, even if Schwarzer hasn't realised it. Three weeks ago, he produced his usual refrain - there are no worthy challengers for his jersey. "I look at who is there to challenge me, and at this moment in time I don't see anyone doing it," he told The Sun-Herald.

There was a time when that argument had some merit. For too long there were too many overseas-based keepers not getting enough game time, giving Schwarzer an advantage. But the advent of the A-League has altered the dynamic. Covic heads a list of contenders who are playing well, every week, before big crowds in a professional league.

The gloves are off, literally, in the race to see who will stand between the posts for the 2010 World Cup campaign. Covic is pushing to get a head start in next month's opening game against Qatar. At the moment he's part of the train-on squad. Schwarzer is tipped to be one of the European-based players recalled for the match. It's going to be a fascinating duel.

"I want to start the Qatar game," says Covic. "I see this as my time. Everyone sets themselves goals, and mine is to be between the sticks against Qatar. I aim to put as much pressure on Schwarzer as I can."

That's where the mind games come in. It's history now that going into the 2006 World Cup finals, Guus Hiddink was undecided who his first-choice keeper would be. That led to a tense stand-off between Schwarzer and his perennial understudy, Zeljko Kalac. It's also history that when Kalac finally got the chance to define his career, he got the wobbles against Croatia.

Schwarzer won the battle of wills, and that's to his credit. Mental strength is a crucial ingredient at the top end.

Covic, who was third-choice keeper at the World Cup, watched from close quarters as the drama unfolded. The sense is he has learnt from the episode. Covic came home from a successful spell in Sweden with Hammarby to join the Jets not because it was a step backwards, but because he felt it would be a step forward for his international career. With new Socceroos coach Pim Verbeek in the stands every week, watching him produce saves such as last weekend's incredible stop to deny John Aloisi, he might be proved right.

The respect for Schwarzer is there, but the desire to usurp a rival who is three years older is driving Covic.

"I've been with him in camp for a long time, he's a very determined guy, he's been playing in top leagues for a long time, and been doing his job at national team level and club level, so I know he's a good keeper - I know what he's capable of," says Covic.

"But he's getting on a bit, and I'm at a good age and I think I'm a quality keeper as well. As I've said before, I've come back from Europe not because of lack of interest, because I definitely had options to stay. It hasn't made me a lesser keeper playing here. I'm the same keeper as when I left Sweden. I was doing a good job over there, and I'm doing a good job here. I've got the ability to play for the national team - if I wasn't confident I wouldn't deserve to be there. But I am confident, and the Qatar game is definitely something I want to be part of. I'm not going to be satisfied being No.2 or No.3."

That Verbeek is the first foreign Socceroos coach to live in Australia means he gets to keep tabs on the A-League candidates. Covic believes close eyes, and fresh eyes, work in his favour.

"Having [Verbeek] here definitely helps," he says.

"The last couple of games, I've been pretty solid, and I've been pretty consistent all season. The more he comes to games, the more I put in good performances, the more it helps me cement my spot. He's come in and he's looking from scratch. Obviously he's got information from other people, but at the end of the day what he sees will make up the team. He's the boss, he's the one who picks it, and that suits me."

Which means last week's stunning stop to deny Central Coast an equaliser, which would have taken Newcastle's top-four hopes down to the wire, couldn't have been better timed. Covic was going one way and Aloisi's header the other, but he stuck out a big left hand to somehow tip the ball onto the crossbar, and the rebound was cleared to safety.

"It's up there with my best," he says. "It was even better considering what it meant, that we got the three points to take us into the finals. I saw the ball the whole way, I was balanced enough to get a hand on it, and it hit the bar. In the scheme of things, it was one of my best for a long time, and probably the most crucial of the year. It's definitely one save I'll remember."

So, surely, will Verbeek.
