Killer tiger's wall was 'not high enough'


By Laura Clout and agencies
Last Updated: 2:56am GMT 29/12/2007

The director of a zoo where a teenager was killed by an escaped tiger has admitted that the wall around the animal’s enclosure was well below the height recommended by a regulatory body.

 
Police launch investigation into death

San Francisco Zoo director Manuel Mollinedo also said that initial investigations suggested that the 160kg Siberian tiger leaped or climbed out of its open-air enclosure, perhaps by grabbing onto a ledge.

"She had to have jumped," he said. "How she was able to jump that high is amazing to me."

According to the Association of Zoos & Aquariums (AZA), the walls around a tiger exhibit should be at least 16.4ft high. But Mr Mollinedo said tiger enclosure's perimeter was 12ft, 5 in with what he described as a "moat" 33 feet across.

He said safety inspectors had examined the 1940 wall and never raised any concerns about its size.

"When the AZA came out and inspected our zoo three years ago, they never noted that as a deficiency," he said. "Obviously now that something’s happened, we’re going to be revisiting the actual height."

The animal, a female named Tatiana, escaped near closing time on Christmas Day, mauling three visitors before it was shot to death by police.

Carlos Sousa Jr., 17, died from a slash to the throat. Brothers Paul Dhaliwal, 19, and Kulbir Dhaliwal, 23, received severe bite and claw wounds.

 
Tatiana attacked a zookeeper last year

Based on earlier incorrect estimates of the height of the enclosure, animal experts expressed disbelief that a tiger in captivity could have made such a spectacular leap. But as the true measurements emerged, they concluded such a jump could have been possible, particularly if the animal was angry.

AZA spokesman Steven Feldman said that the minimum height is just a guideline and that a zoo could still be deemed safe even if its wall were lower.

Earlier reports suggested the victims had taunted the tiger and dangled a limb over the side of the moat. But police said they had no evidence to support this.

However, they admitted they were checking a shoeprint found on the fence surrounding the enclosure against the shoes of the three victims.

Meanwhile the grieving parents of the dead teenager criticised safety measures at the zoo.

"They didn’t do enough for the public, because I think the zoo should be protective on both sides, protective for the people and protective for the animals," the boy’s father, also called Carlos, told CNN.

The teenager’s mother said Christmas would never be the same for them. "This is supposed to be the day for giving, not to take," Marilza Sousa said. "And he was taken away from me."
