Opposition leader confronts election chief

Article from: Agence France-Presse
  • Font size: Decrease Increase
  • Email article: Email
  • Print article: Print

By Michael Mainville

January 08, 2008 10:28pm

RAISING the stakes in Georgia's post-election standoff, opposition leader Levan Gachechiladze dramatically confronted the ex-Soviet republic's elections chief and demanded his resignation.

"Resign! Otherwise we will protest every day," Mr Gachechiladze yelled at the Central Election Commission (CEC) chairman Levan Tarkhnishvili after opposition leaders burst into the commission's headquarters.

Mr Gachechiladze - shown by preliminary results from Saturday's election to be a distant second behind incumbent Mikheil Saakashvili - accused the elections chief of fraud.

"You are telling lies to the whole of Georgia. We demand the resignation of the liar Tarkhnishvili," he shouted, jabbing his finger at the elections chief.

Mr Tarkhnishvili responded by telling him to take his complaints to official channels or the courts. "This is pressure and threats," he said, in footage shown on Georgian television.

He said the opposition has "all possible tools to act within the framework of law."

Later, at a protest against alleged media bias outside the offices of Georgian Public Television, Mr Gachechiladze addressed a message to Mr Saakashvili and interim president Nino Burdjanadze.

"I will not stop until you kill me," he said.

Justice Minister Ekaterine Tkeshelashvili said the opposition "must prevent illegal actions, such as attempts to threaten the work of the Central Election Commission."

"I call upon the opposition to respect the Georgian people and its choice," she said.

The CEC had yet to release final results of Saturday's vote by Tuesday afternoon (early Wednesday morning AEDT), nearly three days after polls closed.

With about 90 per cent of ballots counted, Mr Saakashvili had 52.1 per cent - clearing the 50 per cent barrier for winning in a single round. Mr Gachechiladze, his nearest competitor, had 24.98 per cent.

Mr Tarkhnishvili declared late Sunday that an unofficial final count showed victory was certain for Mr Saakashvili.

His apparent victory followed a bitter contest against six challengers, who accused the US-educated lawyer of rigging the election in the strategically located country of five million.

They have threatened to hold street protests this week, raising fears of a repeat of the unrest in November that triggered Saturday's snap election. Several thousand opposition supporters rallied in the capital Tbilisi on Sunday.

The opposition claims it has documentary evidence of fraud and has vowed to challenge the results in court. Another opposition leader David Usupashvili also said that it was "more than unusual and highly suspicious" that complete results have not yet been announced.

Mr Saakashvili - whose main goal is to wrest Georgia from Russia's influence and win NATO and EU membership - says that if his renewed five-year mandate is confirmed he will pursue free-market and democratic reforms.

The opposition supports the country's pro-Western course, but accuses Mr Saakashvili of becoming increasingly authoritarian and ignoring the poor in pushing free-market reforms.

Despite opposition allegations of fraud, the conduct of the election got a vote of confidence from the foreign observers of the Organisation for Security and Cooperation in Europe (OSCE).

Mr Saakashvili called the election a year early to try to defuse tension that erupted in November when police brutally beat anti-government protestors and he declared a nine-day state of emergency.

The violence disappointed some Western leaders and appalled many ordinary Georgians. But analysts said the election did much to restore Mr Saakashvili's standing in the West.
