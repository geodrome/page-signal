MOVIE REVIEW - Murder and morals collide in Allen’s ‘Dream’


Tom Wilkinson, left, is the rich uncle seeking a most unpleasant favor from nephews Ewan McGregor, center, and Colin Farrell in Woody Allen’s “Cassandra’s Dream.” (Photo courtesy of The Weinstein Company)

By AL ALEXANDER
The Patriot Ledger

Woody Allen’s latest morality tale, ‘‘Cassandra’s Dream,’’ is very much the European version of ‘‘Before the Devil Knows You’re Dead,’’ as brothers desperate for cash enter into a pact with deadly consequences.

While not nearly as accomplished as Sidney Lumet’s much-praised Oscar contender, it’s considerably less off-putting thanks to a pair of appealing performances by Colin Farrell and Ewan McGregor, well cast as siblings with wildly divergent moral compasses but identical wants for quick-strike riches.

They hit the jackpot, too, when their stinking rich uncle (Tom Wilkinson) from the States drops in on them - with the kind of timing that only happens in movies - seeking a most unpleasant favor in exchange.

True to the title, a reference to the Greek goddess of doom, this bit of immoral quid pro quo is headed for a very bad place, turning brother against brother in a suspenseful, twist-filled framework that’s more Hitchcock than Allen.

It’s a genre in which Allen fared much more strongly in with ‘‘Match Point,’’ but given the sorry state of his recent comedies, ‘‘Cassandra’s Dream’’ almost seems refreshing simply because it doesn’t stink.

It’s also nice to see Allen being less predictable and more open to change, like his decision to forgo his usual jazz scores for one by Philip Glass.

The move to Europe, where each of his last three films were shot, also seems to have rejuvenated the auteur, albeit out of necessity after his well of American investors ran dry.

Never, though, has he worked with a cast minus a single American actor.

It’s a bit disorienting at first, but a decision you wind up applauding, given the caliber of the performances by his three male leads.

They keep the thing afloat, but sometimes just barely, as Allen’s moralizing becomes almost as predictable as the plot.

Hitchcock he’s not.

But he is a competent storyteller, never failing to hold interest, even when the story puts credulity severely to the test.

What’s most interesting about it is how closely it follows the plot of ‘‘Before the Devil Knows You’re Dead,’’ with disparate brothers, one of them slick and amoral, the other gullible and honest to a fault, suddenly finding themselves in need of serious cash; a need so severe they’ll literally do anything to get it.

Of the two, McGregor has the more charismatic role, playing Ian, the proverbial player who’s all talk and no cash, a liability that becomes dire after he falls for a beautiful, worldly actress played by newcomer Hayley Atwell.

He’s the epitome of sleaze, but McGregor infuses him with so much charm, no matter how duplicitous it might be, you can’t help empathizing with him.

It’s Farrell, though, who walks off with the movie, delivering a much-needed reminder of the actor he used to be before stumbling through a succession of flops from ‘‘Miami Vice’’ to ‘‘Alexander.’’ As Ian’s younger brother, Terry, a successful entrepreneur with a wife (Sally Hawkins), two kids and a devastating gambling habit.

Even when Terry’s ahead, he can’t quit, a foible that lands him 90,000 pounds in debt to very unsavory people. And as Terry grows more frantic Farrell becomes more compelling, striking at the heart of the guilt that slowly consumes him after he and Ian accept their uncle’s Faustian deal.

As the catalyst for that despair, Wilkinson proves such a charming sociopath that you wish there was much more of him and less of Atwel’s snooty, two-bit actress. But even as limited as the role is, Wilkinson manages to leave quite an impression.

I wish I could say the same about ‘‘Cassandra’s Dream.’’ It’s derivative and instantly forgettable. But it’s no nightmare, either. It’s a Woody Allen film, for gosh sakes, and that should still mean something, even if it is less than a dream.

CASSANDRA’S DREAM
(PG-13 for thematic elements, some sexual material and brief violence.) Cast includes Ewan McGregor, Colin Farrell and Tom Wilkinson. Written and directed by Woody Allen. At Kendall Square, Cambridge. 2 1/2 stars
