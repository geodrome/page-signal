Forget shoot to kill ... just shoot!

Canucks still squeezing stick too hard when it's time to pull the trigger

Jason Botchford, The Province

Published:Â Friday, January 18, 2008

DETROIT -- Hockeytown turned into hockey school for the Canucks.

Last in the NHL in shots on net, the Canucks watched the Red Wings conduct a free seminar in Thursday's first period.

The shot clock after the 20 minutes read Wings 16, Canucks 5.

The Wings shot from everywhere -- the blueline, the half boards and the beer line.

"We talked about it after the first period," Canucks forward Taylor Pyatt said. "They create offence by doing that. They create rebounds and that creates chaos in front of the net. It helped us do a better job in the second half of the game."

At the midway point, it looked bleak for the Canucks. They were down 2-0 and getting outshot 25-8. It seemed like they were in need of divine intervention. They weren't. They just needed some shots.

"The first 30 minutes they really gave us a lesson in how to generate offence," head coach Alain Vigneault said. "These guys do it and they do it constantly and that's why they're top in the league. They just shoot from everywhere. Teams play so well defensively, you have to create scrambles.

"These guys are the best at it right now."

In the second half, the Canucks learned their lesson, changed the momentum and showed they can play with the Wings, at least for a couple periods. They started pelting Wings goalie Dominik Hasek from bad angles and it led to two goals.

Henrik Sedin was able to scoop up a rebound off a Sami Salo slap shot, burying it into the net to cut the Wings lead in half at the 15:21 mark of the second.

The Canucks tallied nine shots in the final seven minutes of the middle frame. By the end of the game, the shots were 49-28.

Markus Naslund, playing in his 1,000th NHL game, scored the tying goal at 18:08 into the second when he did a spin move in front of Kirk Maltby. He took a wild shot that deflected off Wings defenceman Brian Rafalski and into the net.

"We had to figure we can't just stand around and watch them play," Naslund said. "I don't know what our issue has been. I don't know if it's a mindset or what it is. We have to be able to put pucks to the net and it doesn't have to be great chances."

Vigneault expressed some frustration that his team continues to be last in the league in shots.

"You know, we must mention it I don't know how many times a day," Vigneault said.

"For some reason, in the second half of the game our guys started doing it. We got 25 shots I think in the second half of the game. That's one of the best ways to generate offence. Throw pucks to the net, throw them from everywhere and create scrambles."

One of the issues has been a reluctance from blueliners to rip slappers from the point. Sami Salo has received criticism for backing off when he has opportunities. He had one shot against the Wings.

"I think we try to make too many nice plays," Salo offered. "Instead of one guy drive to the net, while the guy up high just shoot the puck. Then, we go for rebounds. It's been a problem all year.

"If you're not going to take your shot when you have the chance, you're not going to get many."
