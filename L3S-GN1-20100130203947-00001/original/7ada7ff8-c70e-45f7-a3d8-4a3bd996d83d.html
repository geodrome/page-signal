Apple bows to pressure for iTunes price cut

By Sarah Laitner in Brussels, Maija Palmer in London and,Kevin Allison in San Francisco

Published: January 10 2008 02:00 | Last updated: January 10 2008 02:00

Apple became the latest US technology company to bow to European regulators when it agreed to cut the prices for music downloads on its UK iTunes website.

The move by Apple to resolve a case brought by European Commission came after Microsoft admitted defeat in its nine-year battle with the antitrust authority in October.

It is also Apple's second concession to European law in the past three months after it was forced to modify the terms of sale for its iPhone mobile device in France to comply with local consumer laws.

Apple said that within six months it would begin to charge UK consumers the same for iTunes downloads as it charges in the eurozone countries. Prices are now nearly 10 per cent higher in the UK, with downloads costing 79p ($1.55) each, compared with €0.99 ($1.45) in countries such as France and Germany.

The price cuts are likely to have little effect on Apple's profits as iTunes accounts for 4 per cent of the company's total sales.

The European Commission will drop its antitrust investigation, which was triggered by a 2004 complaint from a UK consumer organisation.

Since iTunes prohibits users in one European country from downloading music from a website intended to serve another, the Commission complained that this could be considered a restrictive business practice.

The European Union investigated contracts between Apple and Sony BMG, Universal Music, EMI and Warner Music. However, it concluded there was no restrictive agreement between Apple and the companies over the content of iTunes in different countries.

Apple said it had sold music at a higher price in the UK, because some major music labels charge more for distribution rights in the country.

Music copyrights are negotiated on a country-by-country basis. It will now seek to renegotiate these deals.

"Apple will reconsider its continuing relationship in the UK with any record label that does not lower its wholesale prices in the UK to the pan-European level within six months," the company said.

None of the four music majors would comment.

However, analysts said negotiations could prove another test of their already difficult relationship with Apple.
