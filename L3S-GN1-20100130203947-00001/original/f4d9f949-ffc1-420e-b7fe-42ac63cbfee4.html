Emery, McGrattan 'sort things out'

Friendly rivalry. Senators downplay players' punch-up

KEN WARREN, CanWest News Service

Published:Â Tuesday, January 08

Ray Emery, the Ottawa Senators always controversial goaltender, is in the spotlight yet again, this time for fighting

Brian McGrattan near the end of the club's practice yesterday at Scotiabank Place.

It's the latest chapter in a series of incidents involving Emery, who is trying to bounce back from wrist surgery and is aiming to regain the form that helped lead the club to the Stanley Cup final last spring.

While fights at practice occasionally occur in the National Hockey League, they are rare.

Yesterday's battle came after McGrattan cut in front of Emery while they were circling the net during an extended skating drill. Emery hurled some verbal unpleasantries and when the skating stopped, the fists started.

Emery and McGrattan are best friends off the ice - they roomed together when they played with the Senators' AHL affiliate in Binghamton, N.Y. - and they joked afterward about it not being the first time they have pushed each other around.

"It's a little different on a Monday, I guess," Emery said, with a laugh, in the dressing room. "We're big boys, so that's sometimes how we sort things out."

McGrattan, listed at 6-foot-4 and 231 pounds, said the two had a similar exchange while playing in Binghamton in 2003-04.

"That's not our first or second, we've had been in some pushing matches before," McGrattan said, also laughing about the fight. "He can throw them pretty good. I want to keep him sharp, too. You never know when he's going to need to fight."

Emery, listed at 6-foot-2 and

202 pounds, is no stranger to fighting. He dropped the gloves against former Buffalo Sabres goaltender Martin Biron and Sabres tough guy Andrew Peters on the same shift last season, and had eight fights while playing for the Soo Greyhounds of the Ontario Hockey League.

He is so proficient at fighting McGrattan suggests he would rank "in the top five" of all NHL fighters if he was a skater and not a goaltender.

Therein lies the problem.

He is a goaltender and he is coming off surgery on his left wrist. Fortunately for Emery, he was only holding onto McGrattan's collar with his left and throwing with his right.

Yesterday's incident was certainly not a pleasant sight for the tired eyes of general manager Bryan Murray, who only recently returned home from the world junior championship in Prague.

On Dec. 27, the day before Murray left for the Czech Republic, he talked to Emery about an

after-practice episode in which he knocked over a row of water bottles on the bench and then threw his stick into the seats.

The day after, Emery arrived late for practice and coach John Paddock told him to "beat it."

The club has spent all season trying to get the goaltender to improve his work ethic at practice, and there has also been speculation about Emery possibly being traded.

Yet, until Emery showcases the form of a year ago, it's unlikely any team would be willing to pick him up because he signed a three-year, $9.5-million (U.S.) contract last summer.

When Emery struggles, his celebrated off-ice episodes also gain more attention. That includes being involved in a car accident and missing the team's flight before a playoff game against the New Jersey Devils, and his confrontation with an elderly driver at the start of training camp in September.
